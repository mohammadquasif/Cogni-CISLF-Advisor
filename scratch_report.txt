EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
─────────────────
Your firm has a clear ambition for AI-driven credit risk models but is stalled by internal resistance, ambiguous ownership, and talent shortages. The leadership enthusiasm is a strong asset, yet the absence of a coherent strategy and governance creates fragmentation and regulatory vulnerability. The overall strategic posture is one of unrealised potential requiring immediate structural and cultural intervention.

TRANSFORMATION READINESS SCORE: 4.5/10
The organisation has leadership will but lacks the strategic alignment, capability depth, and governance maturity needed to execute AI transformation at scale.

────────────────────────────────────────────────
PILLAR 1: LEADERSHIP MINDSET & VISION
────────────────────────────────────────────────

ASSESSMENT:
Leadership enthusiasm for AI is present but unfocused, resulting in a vision that is aspirational rather than operational. The 18-month delay indicates a gap between strategic intent and the ability to drive change, with risk teams actively resisting rather than being aligned.

STRENGTHS IDENTIFIED:
• Executive team demonstrates genuine enthusiasm for AI transformation.
• Willingness to invest in AI for a core business function (credit risk).
• Recognition that a coherent strategy is needed, indicating self-awareness.

CRITICAL GAPS:
• No clear, communicated AI vision that connects to business outcomes.
• Leadership has not visibly championed the change, allowing resistance to persist.
• Absence of a unified narrative to align risk, IT, and business stakeholders.

STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATIONS:
• Form an AI Steering Committee chaired by the CEO, with the CIO as co-chair, to own the AI vision and resolve cross-functional disputes.
• Develop and communicate a concise "AI North Star" statement linking AI credit risk models to a 20% reduction in default rates within 24 months.
• Mandate quarterly AI strategy reviews with the board to maintain executive accountability and visibility.

PILLAR SCORE: 5.0/10

────────────────────────────────────────────────
PILLAR 2: STRATEGIC BUSINESS-TECHNOLOGY ALIGNMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────

ASSESSMENT:
The unclear ownership between IT and the business is a classic alignment failure, where technology initiatives are pursued without clear business sponsorship or operational integration. The credit risk model deployment is stuck because it is treated as an IT project rather than a business transformation.

STRENGTHS IDENTIFIED:
• The use case (credit risk) is directly tied to a core business function.
• Initial investment in AI technology has been made.
• Business leaders are aware of the potential value of AI.

CRITICAL GAPS:
• No formal business owner for the AI credit risk initiative.
• IT and risk teams operate in silos with conflicting priorities and metrics.
• Lack of a joint roadmap linking AI milestones to business KPIs (e.g., loan approval time, NPL ratio).

STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATIONS:
• Appoint a single business sponsor (e.g., Chief Risk Officer) as the product owner for the AI credit risk model, with the CIO as the technology delivery lead.
• Co-create a joint business-technology roadmap with quarterly OKRs that tie AI model deployment to specific risk reduction targets.
• Establish a cross-functional "AI Risk Squad" with dedicated members from IT, risk, and compliance to break silos.

PILLAR SCORE: 4.0/10

────────────────────────────────────────────────
PILLAR 3: ORGANISATIONAL CAPABILITY & CULTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────

ASSESSMENT:
Limited AI talent and resistance from risk teams indicate a significant capability and culture gap. The organisation lacks the skills to build, deploy, and maintain AI models, and the culture is risk-averse rather than innovation-oriented.

STRENGTHS IDENTIFIED:
• Existing risk team has deep domain expertise in credit risk.
• IT team has foundational data infrastructure.
• Willingness to hire external talent is implied by the challenge.

CRITICAL GAPS:
• No in-house AI/ML engineering or data science capability.
• Risk team lacks AI literacy, leading to distrust and resistance.
• No change management program to address cultural resistance to algorithmic decision-making.

STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATIONS:
• Launch an "AI Academy" for risk and business teams, delivering a 4-week foundational course on AI explainability and model validation.
• Hire a senior AI/ML engineer and a data scientist as core team members, with a mandate to mentor existing IT staff.
• Implement a "shadowing" program where risk analysts work alongside data scientists to build trust and shared understanding.

PILLAR SCORE: 3.5/10

────────────────────────────────────────────────
PILLAR 4: RESPONSIBLE AI GOVERNANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────

ASSESSMENT:
Growing regulatory pressure around explainability is a critical risk that is currently unaddressed. There is no evidence of a governance framework for model risk management, bias detection, or compliance with emerging regulations like the EU AI Act.

STRENGTHS IDENTIFIED:
• Awareness of regulatory pressure indicates proactive risk consciousness.
• Existing risk governance structures can be adapted for AI.
• Compliance team is likely engaged in related discussions.

CRITICAL GAPS:
• No formal AI ethics or governance board established.
• No documented process for model explainability or bias testing.
• No alignment with regulatory frameworks (e.g., SR 11-7 for model risk management).

STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATIONS:
• Establish an AI Ethics and Governance Board with representation from risk, compliance, legal, and IT, meeting bi-monthly.
• Adopt a standardised model documentation framework (e.g., Model Risk Management playbook) that mandates explainability reports for all AI credit risk models.
• Conduct a regulatory gap analysis against the EU AI Act and local financial regulations, with remediation plan within 90 days.

PILLAR SCORE: 3.5/10

MONTH 3 — INTEGRATION (Days 61-90):
• Establish the AI Ethics and Governance Board with first meeting. Owner: CIO & CRO. Outcome: Formal governance body operational.
• Deliver first joint business-technology roadmap with quarterly OKRs. Owner: CIO & CRO. Outcome: Aligned execution plan.
• Implement Model Risk Management documentation framework for the credit risk model. Owner: Head of Risk Analytics. Outcome: Explainability process standardised.

RISK ASSESSMENT
════════════════════════════════════════════════

RISK 1: Continued Risk Team Resistance
Probability: High | Impact: High
Description: Risk teams may continue to resist AI models despite training, slowing adoption and creating shadow processes.
Mitigation: Mandate participation in the AI Academy and shadowing program; tie risk team bonuses to AI adoption milestones.

RISK 2: Regulatory Non-Compliance
Probability: Medium | Impact: High
Description: Failure to meet explainability requirements could result in regulatory fines or model rejection by auditors.
Mitigation: Prioritise the regulatory gap analysis and implement the Model Risk Management framework before full deployment.

RISK 3: Talent Acquisition Failure
Probability: Medium | Impact: Medium
Description: Difficulty hiring senior AI/ML talent in a competitive market could delay the core team build.
Mitigation: Engage a specialist AI recruitment firm; offer competitive equity packages; consider a fractional AI leader as interim.
